\chapter{Mechanical Waves}\label{ch:g12:mechanical-waves}

Saturday evening, a full stadium: a ripple of standing spectators
sweeps around the stands in under a minute, yet nobody leaves their
seat. A pebble's ring crosses the pond while the cork only bobs. This
chapter studies the traveler itself --- the wave --- and the four
numbers that tame it: speed, delay, period, wavelength --- enough to
read an earthquake off a strip of paper.

\section{The disturbance travels, the medium stays}

\begin{definition}[Mechanical wave]\label{def:g12:mechanical-waves:wave}
A \emph{mechanical wave}\index{mechanical wave} is a disturbance
propagating through a material medium --- rope, water, air, rock ---
carrying energy \emph{without transporting matter}: each piece of the
medium swings briefly in place, hands the motion on, and settles back.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[No matter transport]\label{ex:g12:mechanical-waves:transport}
In the stadium wave the ``medium'' is the crowd: what travels is the
act of standing up, while every spectator keeps their seat. On the
pond, the ring expands but a floating cork only bobs in place.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}[Transverse and longitudinal waves]\label{def:g12:mechanical-waves:transverse}
A wave is \emph{transverse}\index{transverse wave} when the medium
moves \emph{perpendicular} to the propagation: a hump races along a
flicked rope while each point moves only up and down. It is
\emph{longitudinal}\index{longitudinal wave} when the medium moves
\emph{along} the propagation: a zone of squeezed coils travels down a
pushed spring, each coil swinging in place.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Sound is longitudinal]\label{rem:g12:mechanical-waves:sound}
Sound is the longitudinal wave par excellence: compressed air ---
higher pressure (\cref{ch:g10:pressure}) --- travels from source to
eardrum; \cref{ch:g12:sound-acoustics} is devoted to it. Earthquakes
send both kinds: P~waves compress the rock, S~waves shake it sideways.
\end{remark}

\section{Speed and delay}

\begin{definition}[Propagation speed]\label{def:g12:mechanical-waves:speed}
The \emph{propagation speed}\index{propagation speed} of a wave is the
distance covered by the disturbance divided by the time taken:
$v = d/t$. It is the speed of the traveling \emph{shape}, not of the
medium.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[The medium sets the speed]\label{prop:g12:mechanical-waves:medium}
For small disturbances, the propagation speed depends only on the
\emph{medium}, not on the shape or size of the disturbance. On a
stretched rope, $v$ increases with tension and decreases with mass per
metre: taut and light is fast, slack and heavy is slow. \admitted
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[Where the formula lives]\label{rem:g12:mechanical-waves:ropeformula}
The rope's exact speed formula is derived in the Year 1 volume; this
year the experimental facts above suffice.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Sound speeds]\label{ex:g12:mechanical-waves:speeds}
Sound travels at \qty{340}{m/s} in air, \qty{1500}{m/s} in water,
\qty{5900}{m/s} in steel --- the stiffer, the faster. Hence the
western-movie ear on the rail: steel announces the train first.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}[Delay]\label{def:g12:mechanical-waves:delay}
A wave of speed $v$ reaches a point at distance $d$ after the
\emph{delay}\index{delay} $\tau = d/v$: two points a distance $d$
apart repeat the same motion, the farther lagging by $\tau$ --- the
echo ranging of \cref{ch:g10:signals-and-waves}, read backwards.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Superposition without interaction]\label{prop:g12:mechanical-waves:superposition}
Where two waves overlap, the displacement of the medium is at every
instant the \emph{sum} of the displacements each would cause alone;
after crossing, each wave continues unchanged. \admitted
\end{proposition}

\begin{example}[Crossing pulses]\label{ex:g12:mechanical-waves:crossing}
Send an upward hump from each end of a rope: where they meet, the
rope briefly rises to the sum of the two heights; then two intact
humps emerge --- as two crossing conversations reach their listeners.
\end{example}

\section{Periodic waves: the double periodicity}

\begin{definition}[Periodic wave]\label{def:g12:mechanical-waves:periodic}
When the source repeats its motion with period $T$ and frequency
$f = 1/T$ (\cref{ch:g10:signals-and-waves}), it feeds the medium a
\emph{periodic wave}\index{periodic wave}: every point repeats its own
motion with period $T$, each lagging its neighbors by the travel delay.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Wavelength]\label{def:g12:mechanical-waves:wavelength}
A snapshot of a periodic wave shows a pattern repeating in space; its
spatial period --- crest to crest --- is the
\emph{wavelength}\index{wavelength} $\lambda$, in metres. A periodic
wave is \emph{doubly periodic}\index{double periodicity}: period $T$
in time at each point, period $\lambda$ in space at each instant.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[The wave relation]\label{thm:g12:mechanical-waves:relation}
A periodic wave of speed $v$, period $T$ and frequency $f$ has
wavelength $\lambda = v\,T = v/f$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
During one period the pattern slides forward by $v\,T$; but the source
--- hence every point --- is then back in its starting state, so the
slid pattern coincides with the original: the repeat distance is $v\,T$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The frequency belongs to the source]\label{rem:g12:mechanical-waves:source}
Passing from one medium into another --- sound from air into water ---
a wave keeps its frequency, which the source dictates, while its speed
changes; by $\lambda = v/f$ the wavelength changes with it.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Concert A, in air and underwater]\label{ex:g12:mechanical-waves:concertA}
The \qty{440}{Hz} concert A of \cref{ch:g10:signals-and-waves} has
wavelength $340/440 = \qty{0.77}{m}$ in air and $1500/440 =
\qty{3.4}{m}$ in water: same note, stretched by the faster medium.
\end{example}

\begin{definition}[In phase, in phase opposition]\label{def:g12:mechanical-waves:phase}
Two points separated by a whole number of wavelengths, $d = k\lambda$,
move identically at every instant: they are
\emph{in phase}\index{in phase}. Points separated by
$d = (k + \tfrac12)\lambda$ always move oppositely --- crest against
trough: they are in \emph{phase opposition}\index{phase opposition}.
\end{definition}

\begin{method}[Reading the double periodicity]\label{met:g12:mechanical-waves:reading}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item On a recording $y(t)$ at a fixed point, read $T$ crest to
        crest; $f = 1/T$.
  \item On a snapshot $y(x)$ at a fixed instant, read $\lambda$ crest
        to crest.
  \item Deduce $v = \lambda/T = \lambda f$; check against a direct
        timing $v = d/\tau$ when available.
  \item Never confuse the two graphs: one axis carries seconds, the
        other metres.
\end{enumerate}
\end{method}

\section{Wavefronts and attenuation}

\begin{definition}[Wavefront]\label{def:g12:mechanical-waves:wavefront}
A \emph{wavefront}\index{wavefront} is a line (or surface) of points
that the wave reaches at the same instant --- a crest line on the
pond. From a point source the wavefronts are expanding circles; far
from any source they are nearly straight. The wave propagates
perpendicular to its wavefronts, successive crests $\lambda$ apart.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Attenuation]\label{def:g12:mechanical-waves:attenuation}
The decrease of a wave's amplitude as it propagates is its
\emph{attenuation}\index{attenuation}. Two causes add up: the energy
of a circular wavefront is shared over an ever-longer crest, and the
medium absorbs a little in passing. Straight wavefronts escape the
first cause --- how a distant storm's swell can cross an ocean.
\end{definition}

\section{Exercises}

\begin{exercise}[$\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:1}
A stadium wave runs the \qty{320}{m} ring of the stands in \qty{40}{s}.
(a) Compute its speed. (b) Does any spectator travel around the ring?
What does? (c) Each spectator stands \qty{1.5}{s} after their neighbor
\qty{12}{m} away: check the consistency.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:2}
Transverse or longitudinal? (a) a pulse on a rope shaken up and down;
(b) a compression sent along a spring; (c) sound in air; (d) a pond
ripple (surface bobs vertically, ring travels horizontally); (e)
seismic P waves (compress along the travel) and S waves (shake sideways).
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:3}
Thunder reaches you \qty{4.0}{s} after the flash. (a) How far is the
strike ($v = \qty{340}{m/s}$)? (b) A diver hears the same delay between
a flash and an underwater boom ($v = \qty{1500}{m/s}$): how far is that
source? (c) What must you know before turning delay into distance?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:4}
Compute the wavelength of a \qty{440}{Hz} sound in air and in water.
When the sound crosses from air into water, which of $f$, $v$,
$\lambda$ change, and which stays fixed?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:5}
On a pond, adjacent ripple crests are \qty{12}{cm} apart and the rings
advance at \qty{0.30}{m/s}. Find the wavelength, the period and the
frequency of the wave.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:6}
A pulse on a long rope is photographed twice, \qty{0.30}{s} apart: its
crest sits at $x = \qty{1.0}{m}$, then \qty{3.4}{m}. (a) The speed?
(b) When does the pulse reach the wall at $x = \qty{7.0}{m}$? (c) A
ribbon knotted at $x = \qty{5.0}{m}$: its motion as the pulse passes?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:7}
Two upward pulses, of heights \qty{3.0}{cm} and \qty{2.0}{cm}, travel
toward each other at \qty{2.0}{m/s} each, crests \qty{4.0}{m} apart.
(a) When do the crests coincide? (b) The rope's maximal height then?
(c) Same if the small pulse is downward. (d) The rope just after?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:8}
(a) Two clotheslines carry the same tension; a pulse is sent down
each. Which is faster: the thin one or the thick one? Why? (b) A
guitarist tightens a string: what happens to the wave speed? (c) Does
flicking twice as hard (a taller pulse) make the pulse travel faster?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:9}
A worker strikes a rail \qty{1.8}{km} away; you listen with one ear on
the steel. Compute the arrival times through the rail (\qty{5900}{m/s})
and the air (\qty{340}{m/s}), and the gap between the two bangs.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:10}
A rope carries a periodic wave of wavelength \qty{24}{cm}. In phase,
in phase opposition, or neither, for point separations (a) \qty{48}{cm};
(b) \qty{12}{cm}; (c) \qty{36}{cm}; (d) \qty{30}{cm}; (e) \qty{6.0}{cm}?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:11}
An earthquake sends P waves at \qty{6.0}{km/s} and S waves at
\qty{3.5}{km/s}, both of period \qty{2.0}{s}. Compute both wavelengths,
compare them with a building, and explain why a whole neighborhood
rises and falls nearly together.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:12}
A recording of the water height at a buoy shows crests every
\qty{0.50}{s}; an aerial photograph shows crests \qty{0.75}{m} apart.
(a) Find $T$ and $f$. (b) Find $\lambda$. (c) Deduce the speed. (d) The
buoy rides a crest at $t = 0$: when next? (e) Is a point \qty{1.875}{m}
away in phase with the buoy, in phase opposition, or neither?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:13}
An anchored buoy is lifted $10$ times in \qty{80}{s} by a regular
swell whose crests are \qty{120}{m} apart. (a) Find the period and
frequency. (b) Find the speed of the swell, in \unit{m/s} and
\unit{km/h}. (c) How long does a crest now at the buoy take to reach
the beach \qty{900}{m} away? (d) Does the buoy drift ashore with it?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:14}
Two identical pulses, one upward and one downward, travel toward each
other at \qty{3.0}{m/s} each, centers \qty{6.0}{m} apart. (a) When do
their centers coincide, and what does the rope look like then? (b) Is
the rope then at rest? Where has the energy gone? (c) What happens
next? (d) Why does ``momentarily flat'' not mean ``nothing there''?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[$\star\star\star$]\label{exo:g12:mechanical-waves:15}
A pebble's ripple spreads on a still pond. (a) Compute the crest
length at $r = \qty{0.50}{m}$ and $r = \qty{8.0}{m}$; growth factor?
(b) The crest's energy is roughly conserved: what happens to the
energy per metre, and to the amplitude? (c) Why does a straight-fronted
swell attenuate far less? (d) And the stadium wave, not at all?
\end{exercise}

\section{Problem: Reading a Seismogram}

\begin{problem}[{Weekend problem --- reading a seismogram: two waves
race out of a broken fault, three circles pin the epicenter, and a
slow third wave gives a coastline its warning}]
\label{pb:g12:mechanical-waves:1}
Night shift at a seismological observatory. At 09:14:32 the needle of
station A jumps: sharp fast wiggles, then slower, wider swings. An
earthquake has broken rock somewhere; two waves race out through the
crust: P waves, compressions at $v_P = \qty{6.0}{km/s}$, and S waves,
a sideways shaking at $v_S = \qty{3.5}{km/s}$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part I --- Two waves, one clock.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item From the descriptions above, classify P and S waves as
        transverse or longitudinal.
  \item Which wave arrives first at every station? Compute the travel
        times of both over $d = \qty{120}{km}$.
  \item Show that the P--S arrival gap is
        $\Delta t = d\,(1/v_S - 1/v_P)$ and compute it for
        $d = \qty{120}{km}$.
  \item Deduce the seismologist's ranging rule
        $d = \dfrac{v_P\,v_S}{v_P - v_S}\,\Delta t$ and show the
        coefficient is about \qty{8.4}{km} per second of gap.
  \item Why does the gap grow with distance --- and why is that a gift
        to a station that never saw the quake start?
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part II --- Circles on the map.}
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Station A measures $\Delta t_A = \qty{25}{s}$. How far is the
        epicenter from A?
  \item Explain why this one reading places the epicenter on a circle,
        not at a point.
  \item Stations B and C measure $\Delta t_B = \qty{15}{s}$ and
        $\Delta t_C = \qty{32}{s}$: their distances to the epicenter?
  \item Explain how the three circles together pin the epicenter down.
  \item The P wave reached A at 09:14:32. Compute its travel time from
        the epicenter, and the origin time of the quake.
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part III --- The wave that crosses the ocean.}
The three circles meet offshore: the fault broke under the sea floor
and set the water column moving. Long waves on deep water travel at a
speed set by the depth --- about \qty{200}{m/s} in the open ocean,
about \qty{10}{m/s} near a beach (admitted here; the formula is
derived in the Year 1 volume). A port lies $D = \qty{600}{km}$ from
the epicenter.
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Convert \qty{200}{m/s} to \unit{km/h} and name a vehicle with
        a comparable cruising speed.
  \item How long does the tsunami take to reach the port?
  \item The port's own seismometer feels the P wave first. After how
        long? If the alarm sounds at that instant, how much warning
        time does the coast get before the tsunami?
  \item At sea the tsunami's period is about \qty{15}{min}. Compute its
        wavelength there, and explain why a ship does not notice it.
  \item Nearing the beach the speed falls to \qty{10}{m/s} while the
        period stays fixed. Compute the new wavelength. What must
        happen to the wave as it bunches up?
\end{enumerate}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Part IV --- Energy, and the report.}
A strong quake releases on the order of \qty{1e15}{J}, carried away by
the waves.
\begin{enumerate}[resume]
  \item Compute the length of a circular wavefront \qty{10}{km} from
        the epicenter, then \qty{210}{km}; by what factor has it grown?
  \item Deduce why shaking weakens with distance even in a lossless
        crust --- and name the second cause a real crust adds.
  \item A liquid cannot resist shear. Far-side stations record the P
        wave but no S wave: what does that reveal about Earth's core?
  \item The high-speed train of the previous year's energy chapter
        (\cref{ch:g11:mechanical-energy}) carries about \qty{1.5e9}{J}
        of kinetic energy: how many such trains is \qty{1e15}{J} worth?
  \item The report, in two sentences: where and when was the quake,
        and how much warning did the port get? Give the three numbers.
\end{enumerate}
\end{problem}
