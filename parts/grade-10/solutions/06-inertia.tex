\begin{solution}{exo:g10:inertia:5}
(a) False: friction stops things; with no force at all the body keeps
its straight uniform motion. (b) True (principle of inertia).
(c) False: it is subject to forces that \emph{compensate} (e.g.\ weight
and reaction). (d) True --- the contrapositive reading of the
principle.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:inertia:6}
\emph{1.} $v = 24/3.0 = \qty{8.0}{m/s}$. Forces: weight and normal
reaction; they compensate (straight uniform motion, friction
negligible). \emph{2.} Friction from the concrete is no longer
negligible: the inventory gains an uncompensated backward force, and

\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:inertia:7}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:inertia:8}
\emph{1.} At rest, tension compensates weight:
$T = 1.8 \times 9.81 \approx \qty{17.7}{N}$. \emph{2.} Each vertical
wire carries half: $T = \qty{8.8}{N}$.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:inertia:9}
Braking: the passenger's body keeps its velocity (inertia) while the
bus loses its own, so the passenger moves forward \emph{relative to the
bus}. Turning: the body tends to continue straight while the bus turns
underneath, so it drifts toward the outside of the curve. In both
cases the ``force'' is fictitious; what is missing is a real force
(grip, handrail) to change the passenger's velocity along with the
bus's.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:inertia:11}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:inertia:12}
\emph{1.} $P = 2.4 \times 9.81 \approx \qty{23.5}{N}$; the vertical
components carry the weight: $2T\cos 40^\circ = P$, so
$T = 23.5/(2 \times 0.766) \approx \qty{15.4}{N}$. \emph{2.}
$T = P/(2\cos\theta)$ grows without bound as $\theta \to 90^\circ$
since $\cos\theta \to 0$; exactly horizontal wires have \emph{no}
vertical component at all, so nothing would carry the weight.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:inertia:13}
Push: weight, reaction, thrust of the hand, small friction; the speed
increases, so the forces do not compensate
(\cref{prop:g10:inertia:converse}). Glide: weight, reaction, small
friction; the stone slows, so again no compensation --- friction is the
uncompensated leftover (on ideal ice it would glide forever). Rest:
weight and reaction alone, compensating
(\cref{thm:g10:inertia:principle}).
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:inertia:14}
\emph{1.} By the principle of inertia, keeping a constant velocity
requires no force at all --- and in interstellar space almost none
acts. Aristotle would predict the probe stops when the ``mover''
quits. \emph{2.} One year $\approx \qty{3.156e7}{s}$, so
$d = 17 \times \num{3.156e7} \approx \qty{5.4e8}{km} \approx
\qty{3.6}{au}$. \emph{3.} Velocity is a vector: changing its direction
changes the velocity, which requires an uncompensated force.
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{exo:g10:inertia:15}
At rest the three forces compensate:
$\vect F_3 = -(\vect F_1 + \vect F_2)$, so $\vect F_3\,(-2, -4)$,
magnitude $\sqrt{4 + 16} = \sqrt{20} \approx \qty{4.5}{N}$, pointing
opposite the resultant of the first two (down-left on the grid).
\end{solution}

\begin{solution}{pb:g10:inertia:1}
\textbf{1.} Straight uniform motion at \qty{0.75}{m/s}. Forces: weight
and the normal reaction of the belt (no friction needed: the suitcase
does not slide on the belt).
\textbf{2.} Yes --- straight uniform motion
(\cref{thm:g10:inertia:principle}). $P = 23 \times 9.81 \approx
\qty{226}{N}$, so $R = \qty{226}{N}$.
\textbf{3.} The diagrams are identical. Rest and straight uniform
motion are one and the same mechanical state: inertia does not
distinguish them.
\textbf{4.} The suitcase keeps its \qty{0.75}{m/s} while the belt
stops: it slides or tips forward until friction absorbs its motion.
Nothing pushed it --- it merely kept the velocity it had.
\textbf{5.} Speeds along the same direction add: $0.75 + 1.2 =
\qty{1.95}{m/s}$. Her velocity is constant, so the forces on her
compensate.
\textbf{6.} $P = 0.160 \times 9.81 \approx \qty{1.57}{N}$ down,
$R = \qty{1.57}{N}$ up, and \emph{no} horizontal force.
\textbf{7.} The puck keeps \qty{8.0}{m/s} with no mover whatsoever.
Everyday surfaces hide friction --- an uncompensated backward force ---
so motion seems to die by itself.
\textbf{8.} Its velocity changes, so the forces no longer compensate
(\cref{prop:g10:inertia:converse}); the culprit is the friction of the
rough ice.
\textbf{9.} No: velocity is a \emph{vector}, and its direction is
changing, so the forces do not compensate --- the ice pushes sideways
on her blades.
\textbf{10.} (a)--(iii), (b)--(i), (c)--(ii).
\textbf{11.} Weight $P = 80 \times 9.81 \approx \qty{785}{N}$, drag
zero: nothing compensates the weight, so the velocity must change ---
the jumper speeds up downward.
\textbf{12.} While drag $<$ weight the forces still do not compensate:
the speed keeps growing, and the drag grows with it.
\textbf{13.} Steady speed: weight and drag compensate, so the drag is
\qty{785}{N}. The motion is straight and uniform --- a falling body in
the exact mechanical state of the suitcase on the walkway: inertia in
disguise.
\textbf{14.} Drag now exceeds weight: the forces do not compensate, so
the velocity changes --- the jumper, still descending, slows down; as
the speed drops, the drag shrinks back toward the weight.
\textbf{15.} Steady again: drag $= P = \qty{785}{N}$ --- exactly the
same as at \qty{50}{m/s}.
\textbf{16.} The terminal drag is always equal to the weight, canopy or
not. The parachute changes the \emph{speed} at which the drag reaches
\qty{785}{N}: \qty{5}{m/s} instead of \qty{50}{m/s}.
\textbf{17.} ``\dots the forces acting on the body compensate (or none
act)'' --- the two readings of \cref{thm:g10:inertia:principle}. Rest
is just $\vect v = \vect 0$, a straight uniform motion like any other.
\textbf{18.} The rink: the puck cruises with no mover in sight. Galileo
points at the ever-smoother surface --- friction, not nature, is what
stops things.
\textbf{19.} Forces do act --- weight \qty{785}{N} down and drag
\qty{785}{N} up; they \emph{compensate}, which is precisely why the
speed is constant.
\textbf{20.} Walkway: weight and normal reaction. Rink: weight and
normal reaction. Parachute: weight and drag. The one law: the principle
of inertia, in its direct and converse readings.
\end{solution}
